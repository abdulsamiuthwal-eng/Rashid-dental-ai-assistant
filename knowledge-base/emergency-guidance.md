## Is This a Dental Emergency?

A dental emergency is any situation causing severe pain, significant dental trauma, uncontrolled bleeding, or swelling that may compromise your airway.

**Signs you may need emergency dental care:**

| Symptom | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Severe, unrelenting tooth pain | Contact the clinic immediately or go to emergency |
| Knocked-out (avulsed) tooth | Act within 30 minutes — see guidance below |
| Significant facial swelling | Seek emergency care — may indicate serious infection |
| Uncontrolled bleeding from the mouth | Apply pressure and seek emergency care |
| Cracked or broken tooth with pain | Contact the clinic as soon as possible |
| Lost crown or filling with significant pain | Contact the clinic — may be urgent |
| Signs of dental abscess (fever, swelling) | Seek urgent dental or medical care |
| Jaw injury or suspected fracture | Go to A&E / Emergency Room immediately |
| Difficulty breathing or swallowing | **Call emergency services (999/112/911) immediately** |

## When to Call Emergency Services

**Call emergency services immediately if:**

- You are having difficulty breathing or swallowing
- There is severe uncontrolled bleeding that does not stop
- You have suffered facial trauma with possible jaw fracture
- There is significant swelling extending to the neck or floor of the mouth
- You are experiencing signs of severe allergic reaction
- You feel dizzy, faint, or unwell in addition to dental symptoms

> **Do not delay calling emergency services if you are unsure about the severity of your situation.**

## Specific Emergency Guidance

### Knocked-Out (Avulsed) Adult Tooth

**Time is critical — act within 30 minutes for the best chance of saving the tooth.**

1. **Handle by the crown** (the white part) — do NOT touch the root.
2. **Rinse gently** with water if dirty — do NOT scrub or use soap.
3. **Reinsert if possible** — gently place the tooth back in the socket if the patient is conscious, calm, and there is no risk of swallowing it.
4. **If not reinserting:** Keep the tooth moist in:
   - Milk (preferred)
   - The patient's own saliva (inside the cheek)
   - Saline solution
   - Do NOT store in plain water
5. **Contact the clinic or emergency dentist immediately.**

> **Note:** This guidance applies to adult (permanent) teeth only. Do NOT attempt to reinsert knocked-out baby (primary) teeth.

---

### Severe Toothache

1. Rinse your mouth with warm water.
2. Use dental floss to gently remove any food that may be lodged between teeth.
3. Do NOT place aspirin or any medication directly on the tooth or gum — this can cause chemical burns.
4. A cold compress applied to the outside of the cheek may help with swelling.
5. Over-the-counter pain relief may be taken as directed on the packaging.
6. **Contact the clinic as soon as possible** — do not ignore severe tooth pain.

> The AI assistant cannot recommend specific medications or dosages. Please consult a pharmacist or medical professional for pain relief guidance.

---

### Cracked or Broken Tooth

1. Rinse your mouth with warm water.
2. Apply a cold compress to the face to reduce swelling.
3. Save any broken pieces if possible.
4. Cover any sharp edges with sugar-free dental wax (available at pharmacies) if causing irritation.
5. **Contact the clinic as soon as possible.**

---

### Lost Filling or Crown

1. If a crown has come off, try to keep it safe and bring it to your appointment.
2. Clove oil (available at pharmacies) applied to the tooth with a cotton ball may provide temporary relief.
3. Dental cement (available at pharmacies) can temporarily protect the area.
4. **Contact the clinic to arrange a prompt appointment.**

---

### Dental Abscess (Infection)

A dental abscess is a serious infection that requires prompt professional treatment. **Do not ignore signs of dental abscess.**

Signs include:
- Severe, persistent, throbbing toothache
- Swelling in the face, cheek, or jaw
- Fever
- Tender, swollen lymph nodes in the neck
- Sensitivity to hot and cold
- Bad taste in the mouth

**What to do:**
1. Do NOT attempt to burst or drain the abscess yourself.
2. Contact the clinic immediately.
3. If you develop difficulty breathing or swallowing, or the swelling spreads rapidly to the neck — **go to A&E immediately.**

> Dental infections can spread and become life-threatening if untreated. Take this seriously.

---

### Soft Tissue Injury (Lip, Tongue, Gum)

1. Clean the area gently with water.
2. Apply pressure with a clean cloth or gauze.
3. Apply a cold compress to reduce swelling.
4. If bleeding does not stop within 15–20 minutes, seek medical attention.

---

### Orthodontic Emergency (Braces)

- **Broken wire:** Cover any sharp end with orthodontic wax. Do NOT cut the wire unless instructed by your orthodontist.
- **Loose bracket:** Contact your dentist or orthodontist for advice.
- **Wire poking mouth:** Use a pencil eraser to push it into a more comfortable position and cover with wax.
- **Contact your dentist** for guidance on urgency.
